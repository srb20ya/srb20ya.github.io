15th June 2000 -

ChuChu Rocket – Thoughts.

 

ChuChu Rocket has finally arrived in Europe, and it’s rather good.

 

Admittedly, for a single player, the game has a limited life-span, the 100 puzzles shouldn’t take you too long to complete (save a few of the Mania ones) and the Stage Challenge is easy enough once you have two players or when you finally master the technique of joy-pad swapping, and the 4P battle against the computer ranges a little too wildly from the computer being utterly moronic (‘Weak’) to being an outright cheat (‘Tough’ – yeah, I’m sure you can move your cursor across the screen in about one second… riigggghttt…), with very little in the middle. But then again – like the excellent Bomberman / DynaBlaster series, it isn’t the single player mode that makes it special.

 

Have some friends round and ChuChu Rocket becomes a lot more fun – especially if you’re playing against an evenly-skilled opponent. Unfortunately, the people in your neighbourhood might be relatively bad at playing ChuChu Rocket or videogames in general – what then? Go online.

 

This is where ChuChu really shines. I had my doubts about the online play feature at first – I was expecting horrendous lag, idiots with the maturity of someone in kindergarten (“u suk ur *** huhu”), Sega of Europe to mess up, and a million other woes. I’ve only ever really had fun playing an online game with Quake and Worms Armageddon – the first of which was good until it was phased out by Quake II, the latter of which was good until you find the servers reek with said idiots, and I was expecting this to be the third one – how wrong I was.

 

Granted – the access time between the servers / game rooms can be a little annoying. On a bad day you can find yourself being stuck for a minute, or even be disconnected – though Sega of Europe have been fixing this. Another thing I found to be very strange is how the game doesn’t hide your password at the start, though only sad, strange people will bother looking through the window to your room and write down your password and imitate you, it’s a little unsettling when it’s displayed on the screen for a good ten seconds at worst.

 

Onto the game rooms – As soon as I got here, I was almost counting how long it’d be before two gamers would yell across to each other with a string of censored out swearing. It didn’t happen, and better still, you can choose not to be subjected to this rubbish if it does happen by pressing the ‘Y’ button, which will open/close the chat window, this though doesn’t stop anyone from ‘whispering’ to you, but it’s nice not to have to enter a game room and hear “buttwhipper2_K” and “wikkiddood” fighting between each other.

 

I created a game room and someone soon hopped in, I waited for any other players to join but I didn’t get the chance – after a short 12 second pause, the other guy started a game, which made me wonder why the heck the Sonic Team didn’t decide to put a ‘Ready’ button in, so that everyone would have to say they were ready before starting a game. On the other hand, this can lead people / game hosts to waste time and never start the game up, but then people would just leave the room. Here – if one person wants to start, everyone else must.

 

The lack of lag is impressive. While at first it might be annoying, you soon get used to having to think ahead and plan a route out – and can actually give you a chance to think. But then this leads to a few more gripes – The Computer. There is no way to disable the option of having / not having a computer player. Not only can they move their cursor around at a far faster speed than a human player can, it seems that they are unaffected by lag. And when you do get lag, it can be pretty nasty… for example:

 

Mouse Mania! You plan out a nice route to your rocket for the ChuChu’s to follow, but find yourself just sitting back and waiting for the arrows to pop up, but by then it’s too late – the Computer has got it, or a lucky human player has had arrows laid to his rocket and is getting a ton of mice. Your route finally gets laid down at this point, which leads to someone getting a pink mouse… Cat Mania! You now have a nice route to your rocket for the cats to follow. You bash the A/B/X/Y buttons in a desperate attempt to get them away from your lovely stock of mice – it’s too late. “Ouch! –120!”, “Ouch! –80!”, “Ouch! –40!”, and you go from 350 points or so to about 50, and to add insult to injury, some more mice have been repelled from your anti-cat route and end up heading to the computer’s rocket.

 

Thankfully – if one player gets lag, everyone else does (except, it seems, for the computer) so everyone is at a disadvantage.

 

Now for the gameplay – it’s fast and furious. If you’re not keeping focused on the task at hand, you can end up with a cat eating away the mice in your rocket, it’s also very enjoyable (save for the lagged periods) and the taunt machine deserves credit – as a replacement for anyone typing “LOL U *** *****R! U GOT CAT!” or “**** U! **** OFF WITH UR *****Y CATS 2 MY ROCKET!” - not that anyone with a typing speed of 80/90 wpm could actually achieve this as the action is so fast, it replaces it with a taunt machine, either gloating in victory or taunt at someone winning – while I wish the cycle speed of the taunt machine can be adjusted, it’s a brilliant way to incite your opponents into carrying on… or disconnecting in frustration, usually the former.

 

Speaking of disconnection, there seems to be a huge annoyance the Sonic Team have overlooked – when people disconnect, it doesn’t just disconnect them from the network, but it seems to disconnect everyone. This means some idiots could start a game and then quickly disconnect – meaning you have to reconnect to the network and waste more money in doing all of this. Why, oh why, oh WHY didn’t they just return you to the game room and make the person who disconnected lose the game? I reckon the majority of people who disconnect are doing so to keep their score intact / because they’re losing and don’t want to achieve victory / to be annoying.

 

Hopefully, all these minor niggles will be fixed – either in the form of upgrade discs, or servers improvements, but these don’t stop it from becoming a good game – nay, a great game. Chalk up another success to the Sonic Team with it’s first (mostly) multiplayer game!
